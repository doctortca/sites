One must not confuse the term "WBT" with "Web site". While WBTs share many common characteristics with Web sites, there is one differentiating factor: WBT's goal is the presentation of information such that learning can occur, whereas a Web site's goal is to present information efficiently, making content easily accessible and searchable.

In WBT, we need to be cognizant of the purpose of the training and our users' level of PC literacy. Therefore, WBT should:

  • limit the amount of information presented on each page, to focus the learner's attention on what is most important: learning the content

  • "guide" the user with a simple navigational structure. (We don't want to provide so many links on a page that a user follows a path and then gets lost.)

  • limit the user's control of their browser somewhat, to ensure that the focus remains on the task at hand. (We don't want users hopping onto e-mail or jumping to another bookmarked Web site in the middle of training.)

  • limit unnecessary interactivity. Unnecessary interactivity refers to that which does not promote learning. (Scrolling and clicking "Next" through page after page of text constitutes unnecessary interactivity. If we dull the learner's senses by having them interact with the PC in this manner, they won't appreciate the "real" interactive moments when they are encountered.)

Compare this to what a Web site aims to do: 

  • permit users to take any path they choose to get the information they need

  • allow users to be in control of the content they are viewing 

  • allow users to obtain information quickly

Keeping these differences in mind, we need to pay close attention to what customers are asking for. Many times what a customer *thinks* they need is training, when actually it is merely information that they want readily accessible to their students. This also applies to our existing content. When converting video content to WBT, it will be useful for us to determine what we have that’s really training and what’s just information. We can then design our interfaces and Web sites accordingly.

There is some middle ground to this debate over instructional design vs. information design. Although they have their differences, the two can benefit from one another. It wouldn't hurt for Web sites to take some lessons from WBT, and vice versa. Instructional designers could create better WBTs by learning more about good information design and architecture. Information designers and Web site architects could also create more effective Web sites by learning more about learning theory and how the human brain perceives and processes information.

Future Trend: Many training solutions will likely constitute a “hybrid” approach.
