Many of the WBTs you'll find on the market today aren't as effective as they could be because they haven't taken into account the principles of adult learning theory. Years of research on learning and on how the human brain processes and retains information has been conducted. That research can be extremely valuable when developing any type of training.

Not every kind of training is suited well to delivery over the Web. 

Most of what we do here at PRIMEDIA is skills training. Skills training can be divided into the following categories. Notice how some are easier than others to implement on the Web:

  • Cognitive skills - These are easiest to train over the Web, and can include the following:

    • memorizing terms and concepts
    • application of rules
    • distinguishing amount items
    • analyzing or synthesizing data
    • evaluating information
    • solving problems

  • Psychomotor skills  - These can be taught on the Web as well, but the implementation is a bit more difficult and costly. These types of skills require complex combinations of physical movement and thought, and can include:

    • engaging in mental or physical activity
    • using muscular actions
    • practicing the skill

  • Attitudinal skills  - These are difficult to train using any type of distance education, but can be accomplished given adequate time and resources. Attitudinal training is that which requires a learner to change his or her opinion about something, and, in turn, his or her behavior. Attitudinal skills training can include:

    • changing attitudes
    • reflecting on personal values
    • exploring other perspectives

If what you're teaching are Cognitive skills, Bloom's Taxonomy is a great resource. Bloom's Taxonomy identifies the following six levels of intellect and cognitive skill:

  • Knowledge - the ability to recall specific and isolated bits of information

  • Comprehension - the ability to understand the information

  • Application - the ability to use the information to do something

  • Analysis - the ability to break down information into parts

  • Synthesis - the ability to put information together to create some cohesive whole

  • Evaluation - the ability to apply judgment about the information for a specific purpose

The first three items above are best for well-structured learning objectives, or those for which there are clear-cut, right and wrong answers.

The final three items are best used for learning objectives that are ill-structured, or those for which there are no clear right or wrong answers. They may involve the application of skills and knowledge to problems that are complex and require a combination of concepts, principles, and theories. They also may require learners to apply knowledge to situations that differ from case to case.

See Selecting a Course Type to find out how Bloom's Taxonomy can help us develop our courses.

To get training to “stick”, we need to support the brain’s “learning cycle”. The learning cycle refers to the way our brains process and retain information. The human brain needs to go through the following cycles (in this order) to learn something new and make it stick:

  •  Motivation: An interesting question or problem will peak our curiosity. Without curiosity or unknowns, we are not compelled to continue a course.

  • Comprehension: Information presented in various media formats helps us to understand what’s being taught.

  • Practice: Practice is key to getting the brain to retrieve previously learned information. The more the information is retrieved, the more likely that information will be retained long-term.

  • Application: The goal in any instruction is to ensure we can apply knowledge or skills. Assessments are a good way to get us to apply what we’ve learned. Ultimately, however, whether we can apply knowledge or skills on the job is the true test of learning.
