In order for us to move at the increasingly fast pace of the Internet, we must modify traditional development methods to meet our customer demands and short timeframes. 

To do this, we propose using a "rapid development" process. This will help us to:

  • Avoid “analysis paralysis”

  • Involve customers early and often

  • Provide successful solutions without lots of development time

  • Move design and development into real-world contexts, whereby testing can occur right away

rapid development methods

To help us achieve the goals above, we propose using the following methods:

  • Condensed "Design, Development, & Test" process (as opposed to the traditional ISD or "ADDIE" model):

    • Lump together the Analyze & Design steps into a shorter "Design" phase, and the Implement & Evaluate steps into a "Test" phase. 

    • Apply good instructional design principles where possible, even though phases are lumped together

    • Keep customer fully involved through the entire cycle, because this eliminates the possibility of miscommunication and unmet expectations 

    • Can still perform *brief* analyses during the Design phase, just keep them high-level (only do what's absolutely necessary) 

  • Development of online "Vertical" and "Horizontal" prototypes (as opposed to detailed storyboards and flowcharts) for an accelerated design phase:

    • Vertical - one entire module completed online

    • Horizontal - whole course shell developed online, but no content

We have a variety of product types that we support. Based on the above methodologies, the following are our proposed processes for the various product types:

  • Straight Media Streams

    (for conversion from "edutainment" & "infotainment" videos)

  • Interactive WBT

    (for conversion from instructional videos)

  • Custom Interactive WBT

    (for custom or original development)

  • WebCasts 

    (for live, simulcast discussions and courses)

Please note: The processes above may vary per project and are dependent on previously defined processes within each network. In addition, if working with vendors, their processes will have to be taken into consideration as well.

One thing to consider when developing WBT as opposed to other types of training is that WBT takes more time to develop. 

Development time is even longer for WBT than it is for CBT. CBT tools are generally slicker and faster for development than the open-architecture WBT tools we must use today. Why is this the case? While open-architecture means greater compatibility between platforms (and tools), it also means the tools don't have the advanced features, functionality, and flexibility on the development end that more proprietary CBT tools have. The same end results can be achieved for WBT, however, but they require considerably more back-end programming and time.
