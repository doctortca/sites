Interactivity is one of the hottest topics among distance educators and trainers. Interactivity can help reduce the feelings of isolation and anonymity that can occur in distance learning. 

Without interactivity, training simply becomes the dissemination of information. There is no corrective feedback or practice to help learners retain and apply the information.

There is much discussion in the industry right now over the "do's and don'ts" of training delivered over the Web. The problem is that while interactivity is often considered an important factor for effective WBT, relatively little has been done to provide trainers with guidelines for creating meaningful interactivity.

Fortunately, there are solutions that can help us overcome the interactivity hurdle:

  • Utilize a framework for interactivity  

University of Houston Instructional Technology professor Atsusi Hirumi, Ph.D., has conducted a great deal of research in the field of WBT, and has developed a framework that greatly simplifies and speeds the process of developing effective interactivity. Dr. Hirumi provides strategies for applying the framework within the context of established systematic design models.

  • Provide variety

Variety in WBT is key to maintaining learners' interest and keeping them motivated to complete courses. Course completion rates are a real problem in WBT today because of the multitude of straight-text courses and page-turners that exist.

So how do we provide variety? By varying the following: 

    • Method (How you’re Instructing): Use practice, scenarios, case studies, simulations, demonstrations, and discussion versus straight presentation of information. 

    • Media (How you’re Delivering): Vary your media by using audio, video, text, graphics, and animations. 

    • Feedback (How you’re Guiding the Learner): Give the learner various constructive hints and suggestions.

Learners need  a variety of constructive feedback so they’ll learn from their mistakes. Merely saying “Correct” and “Incorrect” is not enough. 

    • Givens (What you’re Providing): Exploratory learning and constructive learning (through creating or building something) are more effective than merely *giving* learners informational materials.

Learners will retain more if they discover information for themselves or if they “learn by doing”. (For example, ask a learner to create their own job aid rather than giving them one, and they will retain the information much better.)

  • Borrow ideas from lecture-style instruction

Not all we learned from lecture-style instruction has to go by the way-side! Here are some activity ideas taken from the traditional classroom that we can modify for use in Web learning:

  • Memorizations
  •  Critiques
  •  Problem Solving
  • Reports
  • Projects (can be completed in groups) 
  • Homework assignments
  • Product creation (like creating a job aid)
  • Research (searching for and gathering info on the Web)
  • Questions/answers ("check on learning")
  • Quizzes
  • Case histories
  • Demonstrations
  • Interviews
  • Testimonies
  • Role playing
  • Simulations
  • Games, game shows
  • Debates (chat, message boards, e-mail) 
  • Panels (chat, message boards, e-mail)
  • Dialogues (chat, message boards, e-mail)

  • Force interaction - Sometimes the best way for a person to learn is require them to do a certain thing. Don’t let them skip interactions or page-turn through a course.
