We can only design based on our previous experience. Most WBT developers design training based on all they've ever encountered in training, instructor-directed models (lectures followed by practice).

As a result, when we create WBT, we typically transform instructor scripts and slide-show presentations into mere "page-turners".

It might be unreasonable to expect us to create effective WBT that applies innovative learning strategies when we may never have experienced good examples of them ourselves.

So what should we do? Fortunately, research has been done on various alternatives to the above-mentioned lecture-style approach. Check out these research-based instructional strategies (Adobe PDF document) that can help us develop more effective and captivating online courses. To find out how these strategies can be applied, visit the Interactivity section of this site.

One may think of the traditional CBT format when moving training onto the Web. While this format is great for certain types of training, there are other types of WBT to consider as well. 

The following four categories of WBT exist*. Note how closely these tie to Bloom's Taxonomy:

  • Traditional WBT/CBT (self-study, CBT-style course)

    • description: Provides learners with performance-based training with measurable and well-structured goals and objectives

    • best used for: Training of well-structured topics that require transfer of knowledge, building comprehension, and practicing application of skills

  • Electronic Performance Support System (EPSS)

    • description: Provides learners with "just-in-time" practical knowledge and problem-solving skills. Usually revolves around a search functionality and stand-alone learning objects. Is essentially a "hi-tech" job aid.

    • best used for: Ill-structured problems that require analysis and synthesis, relationships, and organizational principles

  • Asynchronous Virtual Classroom

    • description: Provides group learning and communication. All learners do not have to be online at the same time.

    • best used for: Less-structured problems that require application, analysis, synthesis, and evaluation.

  • Synchronous (Live) Virtual Classroom

    • description: Provides group learning and communication. All learners must be online at the same time.

    • best used for: Ill-structured problems that require synthesis and evaluation of information and shared experiences

*Modified from Margaret Driscoll's Web-Based Training: Using Technology to Design Adult Learning Experiences
