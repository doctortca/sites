The following are some helpful hints and lessons learned from experts in the field:

  • Considerable time can be spent on research (acquiring distance education books, articles, web sites, and other materials), so it is good to gather those resources and place them in a common repository (like our Design Library) for other team members and future staff to refer to.

  • Advice by Margaret Driscoll:

    • "It is easy to lose sight of the importance of the principles of adult education when caught up in the necessary tools and technology inherent in WBT approaches."

    • "Web-based training is about training. This seems obvious, but it is not always clear in many WBT programs. It is too easy to be caught up in the hype and hoopla of the technology and to lose sight of designing effective training. As educators, we must realize that this is just another form of instructional technology."

  • The better you design your courses upfront, the less questions you will get later (thus the less support you will have to provide in the long-run).

  • Many people will say when you increase interactivity in WBT, you invariably increase cost. But consider this: What will happen if you *don’t* include interactivity? Retention and course success will likely be low.

  • Don't expect your SMEs to design the content for you. While they must be involved in course development, they shouldn't be asked to "design" a course alone. SMEs are experts in their respective subjects, but when it comes to instructional design, they:

    • Tend to leave out needs analysis

    • Give little weight to evaluation

    • Often don’t structure the content (but rather use a “throw-up” approach)

    • May not see the big picture

    • May be biased due to their own personal opinions or politics

  • Include a social component in training somehow. A key to learning is often interaction with peers.

  • Adapt instructional strategies to project circumstances

  • Because time frames to develop courses keep getting shorter and shorter, produce “good enough for now” instruction. Improve it later.

  • Choose and use resources (people) wisely - the better quality people you have on your development staff, and the more self-motivated they are, the less work you'll have managing them

  • Avoid common development pitfalls:

    • Moving content from one medium to another is not as easy as it sounds - Customers should be aware that redesign must occur when moving any kind of training between formats (requires rewriting & restructuring content, substituting detail, locating & even recreating original source files, developing interactions, and so forth)

    • Choosing the software tools too soon - The problem here is that you must work in "reverse". Insist on defining the gaps and conducting an analysis of training needs before purchasing tools.

    • Adding a WBT assignment to your already busy workload - Treating a WBT project or pilot like an extra-curricular activity does not do it justice.

    • Underestimating the complexity - In WBT development, there are a large number of dependent tasks and a need for lots of human resources. It also takes longer than most people think to develop WBT.

    • Dedicating too little time to instructional aspects - Developers spend a disproportionate amount of time worrying about technical issues when they should be worried about the design of the instruction

  • Consider the following challenges when taking on large development or conversion efforts (for many courses at once):

    • A great deal of variance can occur among team members in processes and products

    • Frustration can occur if roles and responsibilities of team members are not clarified

    • Logistics (resources, plans, policies, budgets) are often secondary to development. Failure to pay attention to these aspects early on will eventually terminate or severely cripple the entire project.
