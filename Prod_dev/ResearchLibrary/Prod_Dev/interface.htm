While WBT interface design takes into account some aspects of Web site design, it differs slightly because its focus is on learning rather than information access. 

The following are important rules to follow when designing interfaces for WBT:

  • Always do what’s best for the learner 

In WBT, we want learners to focus on content, not on navigating or searching (as they are used to doing in most Web sites). Therefore, it is OK to control browser size, pop up new windows, remove browser controls like forward/back buttons and bookmarking, because this helps keep learners’ focus on content and prevents them from getting lost, distracted, or accidentally jumping out of a course altogether.

  • Provide some form of internal control and flexibility

While we want limit the user's control over their Web browser, we don’t want to insult them. Provide the following navigation aids so that users have some control over the content:

    • Forward/back buttons (custom buttons within the course interface - not browser defaults) and the opportunity to review or to select sections of interest 

    • Pace monitors that tell learners where they are in a course and how much content they have left (ex: progress bars or pace markers like “Page 1 of 10”)

    • More Info buttons, where learners can go optionally for more information on a subject

    • Ability to stop a course and re-enter it at the same point (bookmarking)

Note: While this is desirable, in WBT it usually entails behind-the-scenes database programming. If you want this feature in your courses, weigh the benefits against the additional cost in resources and time this will require.

  • Preserve precious screen space

If your screen is too cluttered (with text, graphics, or any other elements), learners won’t see or learn key information. A significant amount of “white space” is ideal.

 

Make title and navigation bars as small as possible. Navigation and title bars are important to navigating the course, but the most important thing in a course is its content. We want to draw attention to it and not the interface.

  • Reduce amount of text for users to read

Consider the following statistics:

  • We process text on screen about 30% less than text on paper. Therefore, we should use graphics to illustrate points whenever possible.

  • We scan text when online. We only read 1/3 of screen content. Emphasizing important words through bolding, font size, and color is helpful. 

  • We only learn from reading text 10% of the time. Whereas what we see in a graphical and auditory sense combined causes us to learn 50% of the time. Even better is what we experience, which is how we learn 80% of the time.

  • Avoid scrolling (and page turners)

Limit interactivity with the computer to valuable learning interventions like questions, graphics to explore, or simulations.

 

The purpose of interactivity in WBT is to help the learner gain something from the experience of interacting with the computer. If users are having to constantly scroll to read information or click “Next” buttons repeatedly, their interaction with the computer comes to mean little to them. If they are not learning from the interactivity, they will grow numb to its effects.

 

If you need scrollbars, you likely have too much text!

  • Avoid dark-colored backgrounds with light-colored text; they are hard on the eyes

Studies show that reading text on screen is hard enough on the human eye as it is, but light text on dark backgrounds is even worse. Given the low rate of online text reading retention, this combination should be avoided.
